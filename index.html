FEDERAL LAW: PROBABLE CAUSE (Harris)

PARA 1: The Supreme Court in Florida v. Harris established that a dog's alert provides probable cause if the dog is certified by a recognized body or has successfully completed a training program. The "totality of circumstances" is the standard used to evaluate reliability.

PARA 2: Challenges to a dog's reliability based on "false positives" in the field are generally dismissed if the training logs show consistent proficiency. The Court recognizes that dogs may alert to residual odors of drugs no longer physically present.

PARA 3: For the officer, this means the training log is the most vital legal document. It must show "blind" hides where the handler did not know the location, proving the dog was not cued by the handler's behavior.

FEDERAL LAW: HOLDING TIMES (Rodriguez)

PARA 1: Rodriguez v. U.S. states a traffic stop cannot be prolonged for a K9 sniff without independent reasonable suspicion. The stop must end when the traffic mission (issuing the ticket) is done.

PARA 2: If the K9 is on scene, the sniff can occur while the officer is checking the license or writing the summons. As long as it doesn't "add time" to the stop, the Fourth Amendment is satisfied.

PARA 3: Handlers must document the exact timeline: Stop Start, K9 Arrival, and Alert Time. If the alert happens before the ticket is finished, the detention remains legal for a full search.

VIRGINIA LAW: SEARCH & SEIZURE

PARA 1: Virginia courts allow the search of an entire vehicle, including the trunk and containers, based on a definitive K9 alert. This aligns with the federal Carroll Doctrine but is strictly enforced in VA Appeals.

PARA 2: Handlers must describe the "final response" of the dog. In Virginia, a "behavioral change" isn't enough; the handler must be able to testify to the specific alert (sit/stare/scratch) trained for that dog.

PARA 3: Experience is a major factor in VA courts. A handler with your 40 years of experience carries significant weight when testifying about the nuances of a dog's behavior during a search or track.

K9 CPR (INSTRUCTIONAL)

PARA 1: Check femoral pulse first. Lay dog on its right side. Compress 100-120 BPM over the widest part of the chest (for GSD/Malinois). Push down 1/3 to 1/2 of the chest width.

PARA 2: Use "Mouth-to-Snout" breathing. Hold the muzzle shut and blow into the nose until you see the chest rise. The cycle is 30 compressions to 2 breaths. Repeat without stopping.

PARA 3: Rotate handlers every 2 minutes to keep compressions deep. Physical fatigue is the #1 reason CPR fails in the field. Continue until heartbeat returns or you reach the ER.

GUNSHOTS & BLEEDING

PARA 1: Apply immediate pressure. For limbs, use a K9 tourniquet high and tight. For chest/body wounds, pack the wound with hemostatic gauze (QuikClot) and hold for 5 minutes.

PARA 2: Chest wounds require an occlusive seal (chest seal) to prevent lung collapse. If you hear a sucking sound, seal it immediately with plastic or a dedicated seal.

PARA 3: Move the dog to a cruiser and transport with sirens. Do not wait for a vet to come to you. Keep the dog warm to prevent shock and watch for pale gums.
