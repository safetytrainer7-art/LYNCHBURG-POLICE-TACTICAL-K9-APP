K9 FIRST AID

1. K9 CPR

Lay dog on right side. Place hands over the widest part of the chest. Compress 1/3 to 1/2 chest width at 100-120 BPM. Allow full recoil.

Deliver 2 rescue breaths every 30 compressions by breathing into the nose with the mouth held shut. Continue until help arrives.

2. SNAKE BITES

Keep the dog as calm and still as possible to slow the spread of venom. Identify the bite location and keep it at or below the level of the heart; do not use a tourniquet or apply ice, as these increase tissue damage. Do not attempt to "suck out" the venom.

Remove the dog's collar or harness immediately as swelling will be rapid and can restrict the airway. Note the snake's appearance for the vet if safe to do so, and transport immediately to a facility stocked with antivenin.

3. EYE INJURIES

Prevent the dog from rubbing the eye immediately. If a foreign object is seen, flush the eye gently with sterile saline toward the corner; do not use tweezers. If the eye is displaced from the socket, do not push it back in.

Keep the eye moist with saline and a clean damp dressing. This is a surgical emergency where minutes count to save vision. Transport the dog in a darkened vehicle to keep the eye relaxed.

4. POISON / TOXIN

Identify the substance. If it was skin contact, flush with water for 20 minutes. Do not induce vomiting for caustic or petroleum-based toxins.

Keep the dog's airway clear if they are seizing. Bring a sample of the toxin to the vet to assist with rapid identification and treatment.

5. BROKEN BONES

Do not try to set the bone. Cover open wounds with sterile dressings. Muzzle the dog if in high pain to prevent accidental bites during transport.

Splint only if transport is delayed. Support the dog's weight using a belly sling (towel) to prevent further limb damage during movement.
