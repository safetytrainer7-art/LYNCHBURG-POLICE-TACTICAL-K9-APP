LEGAL SHIELD: K9 CASE LAW

1. Rodriguez v. United States (Federal)

The Constitutional Standard: A police stop may last no longer than is necessary to address the 'mission' of the stop (checking license, warrants, and insurance). Prolonging a stop for a K9 sniff without independent reasonable suspicion is an illegal seizure.

Field Application: Handlers must work efficiently. Conduct the K9 sniff while a second officer handles administrative duties to ensure the search remains within the lawful timeframe of the initial stop.

2. Virginia v. Robertson (State)

The Virginia Standard: The Virginia Court of Appeals has ruled that an alert by a certified narcotics-detection K9 provides full probable cause for a warrantless search of a vehicle under the automobile exception.

Field Application: Explicitly document the K9’s training, certification, and the specific 'final response' observed (e.g., a sit or stare) to ensure the search is legally defensible in a Virginia court.

K9 TRAUMA & FIRST AID

Post-Apprehension Medical Duty

Duty of Care: Once a suspect is in custody, officers have a constitutional duty to provide or summon medical aid for injuries, including K9 bites. Failure to act can lead to claims of 'deliberate indifference.'

Field Protocol: Immediately after the 'out' command and handcuffing, evaluate the bite site and apply pressure to bleeding. Document the exact time aid was provided and the arrival of EMS.
