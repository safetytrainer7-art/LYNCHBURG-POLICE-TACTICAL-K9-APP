Tactical K9 First Aid

1. K9 CPR (Advanced Protocol)

Confirm the animal is in full arrest by checking for a femoral pulse in the groin area and observing for lack of chest rise. Lay the K9 on their right side on a hard surface to stabilize the heart. Place your hands directly over the heart (where the elbow meets the chest) and compress 1.5 to 2 inches deep.

Maintain a constant rhythm of 100 to 120 compressions per minute. For rescue breathing, hold the muzzle closed with both hands to create an airtight seal and breathe directly into the nostrils. Deliver 2 breaths for every 30 compressions. Do not stop this cycle until the K9 regains a pulse or you are relieved by veterinary staff.

2. Snakebites (Pit Viper/Timber Rattler)

In the Blue Ridge terrain, venomous bites are a high risk. Immediately restrict the K9's movement; do not let the dog walk, as muscle activity speeds up venom absorption. Keep the bite site below the level of the heart.

Do not apply ice, do not use a tourniquet, and do not attempt to "suck out" the venom. These actions cause more tissue damage. Monitor for rapid swelling and labored breathing. Transport to an emergency facility immediately for Antivenin; call ahead so they can prepare the dosage.

3. Gunshot Wounds & Penetrating Trauma

Apply immediate direct pressure to the wound using sterile gauze or the cleanest cloth available. If the wound is on a limb and bleeding is arterial (bright red/spurting), apply a K9-specific tourniquet high and tight above the wound and lock it. Note the time of application.

If the wound is in the chest cavity, look for "bubbling" blood, which indicates a sucking chest wound. Seal this immediately with an airtight plastic dressing or chest seal to prevent a collapsed lung (Pneumothorax). Keep the K9 warm to combat shock during extraction.

4. Heat Stroke & Exhaustion

Heat stroke is a medical emergency that leads to organ failure. If the K9 has brick-red gums, thick ropey saliva, or a temp over 104°F, begin cooling immediately. Move to shade or an air-conditioned cruiser.

Apply cool water (not ice) to the groin, paws, and neck. Use a fan to create airflow for evaporative cooling. Stop cooling once the rectal temperature hits 103°F to prevent the dog from crashing into hypothermia. Continuous monitoring is required as internal damage can continue after the temp drops.

5. Broken Bones & Orthopedic Injury

Stabilize the limb exactly as it was found; do not attempt to straighten a deformity. If the bone has broken the skin, cover it with a clean, moist dressing to prevent infection. Use a field-expedient splint (like a folded magazine or stick) only if it can be done without causing more pain.

Muzzle the K9 before treating a fracture. Even the most loyal partner will bite when experiencing the intense pain of a broken bone. Keep the K9 calm and immobilized on a flat board or stretcher for transport to prevent further nerve damage.

6. Eye & Paw Injuries

For eye injuries, flush gently with saline or clean water. Do not allow the dog to rub the eye. For paw injuries, check for glass or thorns. Apply a pressure wrap to control bleeding, but ensure it is not so tight that it cuts off blood flow to the rest of the limb.
