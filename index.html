FEDERAL CASE LAW

Probable Cause (Florida v. Harris)

Para 1: The Supreme Court held that a K9's alert provides probable cause if the dog has been certified or has successfully completed a training program. Reliability is judged by training records, not just field performance.

Para 2: Defense challenges based on "false positives" are addressed by the Court acknowledging that dogs identify residual odors. Certification serves as the legal proof of the dog's nose.

Para 3: Operationally, handlers must document all "blind" training to prove the dog is not responding to handler cues. Meticulous logs are required for Federal court admissibility.

VIRGINIA CASE LAW

Tracking & Apprehensions

Para 1: VA courts accept tracking evidence when pedigree and training are proven. Handlers must testify to the dog's ability to follow human scent over distance.

Para 2: Apprehensions use the "Objective Reasonableness" standard. Clear warnings must be given before deployment to allow for surrender.

Para 3: Experience matters—Virginia courts weigh the handler's years of service heavily when interpreting the dog's behavioral changes on a track.

K9 MEDICAL EMERGENCY

K9 CPR PROTOCOL

Para 1: Check femoral pulse. Lay dog on right side. Compress 100-120 BPM over the widest part of the chest (Shepherds/Malinois).

Para 2: Rescue breathing is "Mouth-to-Snout." Seal the muzzle and blow into the nose until the chest rises. Ratio is 30:2.

Para 3: Rotate handlers every 2 mins to prevent fatigue. Continue until heartbeat returns or you reach the vet clinic.
