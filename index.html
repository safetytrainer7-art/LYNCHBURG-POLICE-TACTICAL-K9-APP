K9 FIELD TRAUMA MEDIC

OFFICER SAFETY & K9 LIFE SUPPORT

1. K9 CPR & RESUSCITATION

Full Color Diagram: Cardiac Compression Zone

Immediate Action: Confirm the K9 is unresponsive and not breathing. Lay the K9 on their right side on a firm surface. For medium to large dogs, place your hands over the widest part of the rib cage. For deep-chested dogs like GSDs, place hands directly over the heart. Compress the chest 1.5 to 2 inches at a rate of 100-120 beats per minute. Ensure full chest recoil between compressions to allow the heart to refill with blood.

Ventilation: Close the K9's mouth tightly with your hands and exhale directly into the nose until you see the chest rise. The ratio is 30 compressions to 2 breaths. Continue this cycle without interruption until the K9 regains consciousness or you reach a veterinary facility. Do not stop for more than 5 seconds. If a second officer is available, rotate every 2 minutes to prevent fatigue and maintain high-quality compressions.

2. GUNSHOTS / SUCKING CHEST WOUNDS

Full Color Diagram: Occlusive Dressing Placement

Gunshot Trauma: Immediately locate entry and exit wounds. For extremity hits, apply a K9-specific tourniquet or high-pressure dressing above the wound. If the wound is in the torso, apply immediate direct pressure with sterile gauze or the cleanest cloth available. Pack the wound if it is in a "junctional" area like the shoulder or hip where a tourniquet cannot be used.

Sucking Chest Wound: If you hear a "hissing" sound from the chest, it is an immediate life threat. Apply an occlusive (air-tight) dressing immediately—use a chest seal or plastic wrap taped on three sides. This allows air to escape the chest cavity but prevents it from entering, stopping a collapsed lung (pneumothorax). Monitor for labored breathing and transport at high priority.
