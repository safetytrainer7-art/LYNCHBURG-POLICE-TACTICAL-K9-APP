NARCOTICS & SAFETY

SECTION I: IDENTIFICATION

Field identification requires a "Detect, then Inspect" approach. Common drugs in VA range from "Ice" methamphetamine to "Grey Death" fentanyl mixtures. Look for tell-tale packaging like "baggies" or wax paper "stamps," which indicate high-potency synthetics.

Synthetic opioids like Fentanyl are now frequently pressed into blue "M30" pills. These are often inconsistent in color and chalky. Handlers must assume every pressed pill contains a lethal dose and use nitrile gloves immediately.

Distribution (PWID) hinges on scales, cash, and "corner-cut" baggies. In Virginia, multiple individual units are primary evidence for distribution, even with low total weight. Document paraphernalia separately to build the full case.

SECTION II: HAZMAT & EXPOSURE

The "Blue-Line" Protocol is the standard for Fentanyl exposure. Move K-9s upwind immediately and clear residue with a wet cloth—never use air blowers. Do not field test loose powders; double-bag and transport to a lab.

Symptoms include respiratory depression and pinpoint pupils. Administer Narcan immediately; high-potency synthetics may require 4-5 doses. If you feel "heavy" or "off," notify dispatch for EMS immediately.

Decontaminate equipment with soap and water. Avoid bleach or alcohol cleaners on fentanyl, as they can increase skin absorption. Document exposure in the "Officer Safety" log, noting time and PPE worn.

K9 LEGAL BIBLE

Section I: Federal Standards

The Fourth Amendment establishes that a K-9 sniff in a public place is not a "search." Rulings like US v. Place clarify that dogs are "sui generis" because they only disclose contraband presence.

The "Clock" is critical per Rodriguez v. US. A stop cannot be extended beyond the initial traffic mission without independent Reasonable Suspicion to hold the vehicle.

Florida v. Harris held that if a dog is certified by a reputable organization, its alert provides Probable Cause. Handlers must maintain training logs to show the dog is "well-trained."

Section II: Virginia Specifics

Virginia follows the "Plain Sniff" doctrine. A K-9 alert is sufficient for a warrantless vehicle search under the Automobile Exception. Once a dog alerts, you have immediate Probable Cause.

The Curtilage Rule is strictly enforced. Bringing a dog onto a porch without a warrant or consent is a trespass. Handlers must stay in public areas to avoid evidence being suppressed.

Brown v. Commonwealth reinforces officer testimony. If the handler can articulate the dog's body posture and final alert, Virginia courts typically uphold the search.
