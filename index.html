K9 Tactical First Aid

1. K9 CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation)

Step 1: Check for Pulse. Feel for the femoral artery on the inner thigh. If no pulse is detected, lay the K9 on their right side on a firm surface.

Step 2: Compressions. Place your hands over the widest part of the rib cage. Depress the chest 1.5 to 2 inches for 100 to 120 beats per minute. Ensure full chest recoil between compressions.

Step 3: Rescue Breaths. Close the K9's mouth tightly with both hands. Deliver 2 breaths into the nostrils every 30 compressions. Continue until the K9 breathes on their own or you reach a vet.

2. Snakebites (Pit Vipers/Copperheads)

Immediately immobilize the K9 to slow venom circulation. Do not apply a tourniquet, do not cut the wound, and do not apply ice. Keep the bite site at or below heart level. Identify the snake species if possible from a safe distance and transport immediately for antivenin treatment.

3. Eye Injuries (Brush/Debris)

Flush the eye gently with sterile saline solution or clean water to remove debris. Do not allow the K9 to rub their face on the ground or with their paws. If an object is impaled in the eye, do not remove it; stabilize it with a protective cup or bandage and transport immediately.

4. Paw Injuries (Lacerations/Tears)

Examine pads for glass, thorns, or debris. Clean the area with antiseptic. Apply a pressure bandage to control bleeding. In the rugged terrain of Amherst or Nelson, ensure the bandage is secure but not cutting off circulation. Use a boot or wrap to keep the wound clean during extraction.

5. Heat Exhaustion

Watch for heavy panting and "spatulate" tongue (tongue flattened out). Move the K9 to a shaded, air-conditioned area. Offer small amounts of water. Wipe down the belly and inner thighs with room-temperature water. Monitor closely to ensure it does not progress to heat stroke.

6. Heat Stroke (Critical)

Signs include brick-red gums, seizures, or collapse. Rectal temperature over 104 degrees is a crisis. Apply cool water to the paws, groin, and neck. Use a fan for evaporative cooling. Stop cooling once the temperature reaches 103 degrees to prevent hypothermia. Emergency vet transport is mandatory.

7. Broken Bones (Fractures)

Minimize movement. Do not attempt to "set" the bone. If the fracture is open (bone showing), cover with sterile gauze. Splint the limb only if transport will be prolonged, ensuring the splint extends above and below the break. Use a muzzle if the K9 is in extreme pain to prevent accidental bites.

8. Excessive Bleeding (Hemorrhage)

Apply direct pressure with clean gauze. If bleeding through, add more gauze without removing the first layer. For life-threatening limb bleeds, apply a K9 tourniquet high and tight. Document the time of application. For torso wounds, pack the wound with hemostatic gauze and maintain pressure.

9. Gunshot Wounds

Treat for hemorrhage first. If the wound is in the chest and you hear a "sucking" sound, seal it immediately with plastic wrap or a chest seal to prevent lung collapse. Keep the K9 warm to prevent shock and transport to a trauma center immediately. Monitor for labored breathing.

10. Bites & Stings (Insects/Animals)

Remove stingers by scraping with a card—do not use tweezers which may squeeze more venom. Monitor for allergic reactions (facial swelling, hives). If the K9 has difficulty breathing, it is an anaphylactic emergency. For animal bites, flush with water and bandage loosely; watch for infection.
