K9 TACTICAL FIRST AID

1. K9 CPR (Hands-Free Instruction)

Lay the dog on their right side on a firm surface. For most dogs, place your hands over the widest part of the chest; for barrel-chested dogs like Labs, place hands directly over the heart. Compress the chest 1/3 to 1/2 of its width at a rate of 100-120 beats per minute, allowing full recoil between compressions.

Every 30 compressions, deliver 2 rescue breaths by closing the dog's mouth tightly and breathing directly into the nose until the chest rises. Continue this cycle without interruption until the dog regains consciousness or you reach a veterinary facility. Using a metronome or a steady rhythm is vital for effectiveness.

2. GUNSHOT WOUNDS

Immediately identify entry and potential exit wounds. If the wound is in the chest cavity (a sucking chest wound), seal it immediately with plastic or a vented chest seal to prevent lung collapse. For limb wounds, apply a tactical K9 tourniquet high and tight above the wound if life-threatening bleeding is present.

Keep the dog warm to prevent shock and maintain a clear airway. Do not attempt to probe the wound or remove bullet fragments in the field, as this can cause catastrophic secondary hemorrhaging. Rapid transport to a trauma-capable vet is the only priority after the bleeding is packed.

3. EXCESSIVE BLEEDING (HEMORRHAGE)

Apply firm, direct pressure to the source of the bleed using sterile gauze or the cleanest cloth available. If the blood soaks through, do not remove the original dressing, as you will tear away forming clots; simply add more layers on top and increase pressure. Use a pressure bandage to lock the dressing in place.

For arterial bleeds on the tail or limbs that cannot be controlled with pressure, use a K9-specific tourniquet. Monitor the dog's gum color; if gums turn pale or white, the dog is entering hypovolemic shock and requires immediate fluid resuscitation by a vet.

4. HEAT EXHAUSTION

Move the dog to a shaded area or air-conditioned patrol unit immediately. Douse the dog’s body with cool (not ice-cold) water, focusing specifically on the paw pads, groin, and underarms where heat exchange is most effective. Use a fan to provide evaporative cooling while monitoring the dog's responsiveness.

Stop active cooling once the dog's temperature reaches 103 degrees to avoid over-cooling and rebound hypothermia. Never force the dog to drink water if they are semi-conscious, as this can lead to aspiration pneumonia. Seek vet care even if the dog appears to recover, as internal organ damage can be delayed.

5. SNAKE BITES

Keep the dog as calm and still as possible to slow the spread of venom through the lymphatic system. Identify the bite location and keep it at or below the level of the heart. Do not use a tourniquet, do not apply ice, and never attempt to "suck out" the venom, as these methods increase tissue necrosis.

Note the snake's appearance if possible for anti-venom selection, but do not risk a second bite to capture it. Swelling will be rapid and severe; remove the dog's collar or harness immediately before it restricts the airway. Get to a vet with antivenin stock immediately.
